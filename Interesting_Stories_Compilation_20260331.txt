Interesting Stories from the Lake Worth Newspaper Archive
Compiled: 2026-03-31
Source: 1,784 articles from Fort Worth Star-Telegram and Record-Telegram (1913-1918)

========================================
I. ORIGIN STORIES: Naming the Lake
========================================

1. [1913-07-05] "MINNETONKA IS NAME OF LAKE AT RESERVOIR"
The lake's first name was "Lake Minnetonka," suggested by dam superintendent J.C. Lord -- meaning "Great Water" in an Indian tongue. Officials were already dreaming of canoes, pleasure resorts, and extending the streetcar line.

2. [1913-10-28] "NEW NAME IS SOUGHT FOR RESERVOIR LAKE"
N.H. Lassiter of the Chamber of Commerce objected to borrowing a Minnesota lake's name and proposed "Panther Lake" or "Panther City Lake." Mayor Milam appointed a naming committee including newspaper editors.

3. [1913-12-06] "NEW RESERVOIR TO BEAR NAME OF LAKE WORTH"
After considering "Panther Lake," "Lake Jarvis," "Lake Superior," "Milam," and "Davis," they chose "Lake Worth" because it "would best serve the interests of all and at the same time advertise the name of Fort Worth throughout the land." The committee also recommended banning hunting and restricting clubhouse construction.


========================================
II. DRAMATIC DEATHS & NEAR-DROWNINGS
========================================

4. [1914-08-22] "Two Men Drown and Boy Narrowly Escapes Death Below New Dam"
H.W. Kendrick and Oscar Hamm drowned in a "suckhole" near the spillway, while teenager Wesley Cleveland barely survived. The entire community shut down for the funeral.
  Follow-up: [1914-08-25] "BUSINESS SUSPENDS DURING FUNERAL"

5. [1917-06-09] "Laugh Over Joke Rocks Boat and Man Is Drowned"
Fred Meeks, 45, and his companion Jack Donnelley were standing up in a boat telling jokes. Violent laughter rocked the boat, capsizing it. Nearby boatmen saved Donnelley but Meeks's body wasn't found until 10:30 that night after a two-hour search.

6. [1917-10-01] "BOY LOSES LIFE IN VIEW OF MEN ON BANK OF LAKE"
Harry Johnson, a 16-year-old orphan, drowned 150 feet from shore in 35 feet of water while a party of fishermen that included the county sheriff watched helplessly from shore. Several men rushed into the water to swim to him, but he sank before they could reach him.

7. [1917-07-30] "GIRLS STRUGGLE IN BEACH; ONE BARELY ESCAPES DROWNING"
Three young women were in the water when one started to dive but lost her balance and fell backward, dragging her companions under. Miss Dorothy Thompson was taken under twice and was unconscious for an hour before being resuscitated.

8. [1915-11-26] "NINE RESCUED ON LAKE WORTH BY CARETAKER"
Caretaker Andy Hartshorn and his son rescued nine people from three separate parties in a single day when high winds made the lake dangerously choppy. One 14-year-old boy was minutes from drowning.
  Quote: "Unless this precaution is taken a drowning is liable to occur at any time. The lake is big and very deep."

9. [1918-01-21] "HAND HE SHOT OFF 33 YEARS AGO CAUSES DUCK HUNTER'S DEATH"
Charles Riedel, 51, had lost his right hand in a hunting accident near Chicago 33 years earlier. While duck hunting on Lake Worth, he slipped on ice in the bottom of his boat and couldn't catch himself with his missing hand. His shotgun discharged into his stomach.
  Quote: "Look what I've done," was all that Riedel said when he sank to the bottom of the boat, dying.


========================================
III. THE AVIATOR & THE BALLOONIST
========================================

10. [1914-01-12] "AVIATOR HAS SECOND FALL IN TWO SUNDAYS"
R.G. King fell more than 100 feet through high-tension electric wires, demolished his Benoist biplane, and walked away with only a scratch on his ear. "It is thought the amount of electricity in the wires saved King's life by burning up the connection quickly." He promised to fly again next Sunday.

11. [1914-08-10/08-16] The Saga of Mme. Aerida's Balloon
A female aeronaut named Mme. Aerida made balloon ascensions over Lake Worth and Lake Como. Her balloon escaped and was lost, prompting a county-wide search. It was finally found two and a half miles northwest of Lake Worth.


========================================
IV. COLORFUL CHARACTERS & HUMAN INTEREST
========================================

12. [1914-05-08] "Escaped Convict, Remorseful, Would Pay for Own Capture"
John Key, serving time for bigamy, walked away from a prison farm on Christmas Day when "a longing for home came over him." He found work at Lake Worth, but his conscience tortured him. When captured, he insisted on paying the $25 reward himself out of his $52 in wages. The state wouldn't let him.
  Quote: "I had rather be allowed to pay this reward myself. It is wrong to make the state pay it."
  The prison agent didn't bother putting handcuffs on Key when taking him back to Huntsville.

13. [1917-06-06] "FIRST MATRON FOR LAKE BATHING PAVILION IS BORDER REFUGEE; ESCAPED FROM VILLA RAIDERS"
Mrs. Alice Hunter, hired as beach matron, was a rancher who had been chased by 80 of Pancho Villa's bandits near the Mexican border. She escaped only because she was in a car and they were on horseback.
  Quote: "I merely will expect everyone to act as ladies and gentlemen. Those who cannot behave themselves will be asked to do me the favor of leaving."

14. [1915-04-21] "A NEGRO HERE REMEMBERS SAN JACINTO"
John Hickman, a 97-year-old Black man living on a farm near Lake Worth, personally remembered the fall of the Alamo. He had been body servant to one of its defenders and "obtained and buried his master's body." His mind was still clear and he still worked his farm actively.

15. [1918-04-28] "Seeks to Emulate Huck Finn but Mother Interferes"
A boy from Dallas camped alone on the shores of Lake Worth for two weeks, living off food given by campers. He told everyone his mother was dead and that he wanted to "live in the open like Tom Sawyer, Huck Finn and other boy pirates." His mother, very much alive, was eventually located.

16. [1918-03-13] "SPREAD OF FOOD CONSERVATION SOUNDS DEATH KNELL OF HOBO"
A released prisoner from the Lake Worth workhouse lamented that wartime food conservation was destroying the hobo way of life. No more decent "handouts" from back doors.
  Quote: "Even the dogs have a meaner disposition; try and ready to grab you; must be underfed, too."


========================================
V. QUIRKY STORIES & TALL TALES
========================================

17. [1914-06-18] "Shakes Dice From Fish's Maw, Eleven Falls--Natural Pass"
Ben Wilson of North Fort Worth caught a bass and found two dice in its stomach. When shaken out, they rolled an eleven -- "natural pass." The reporter spins an elaborate literary fantasy about mermaids playing craps at the bottom of the lake.
  Quote: "Is it possible that mermaids abound in the waters about Lake Worth? If there are mermaids in that locality perhaps it can be partly explained. The mermaids might have been engaged in a crap game and got careless enough to leave them where some meddlesome fish could reach them."

18. [1917-02-13] "BATTLE WITH WOLVES THRILLING -- And It All 'Happened' Near Lake Worth"
The Chicago Saturday Blade published an outrageous tale of four Fort Worth duck hunters chased across the frozen lake by thirty timber wolves. The Star-Telegram reprinted it with barely concealed mockery.
  Quote: "While trying to pry our boat loose from the ice we found the ice would hold us. So we walked out on it and had made about half the distance across the lake when we looked back -- and there was the whole pack of about thirty wolves in full pursuit."

19. [1917-08-05] "LEADING MULE INTO LAKE COSTS $37.50"
A.J. Stallings of Dallas, possibly motivated by "humanitarian" concern, led J.C. Rider's mule into Lake Worth for a bath. The mule drowned. A judge set damages at $37.50 after both sides brought veterinarians and commission men to testify about the mule's market value.

20. [1917-10-05] "'Panther City' Slides Under Bridge After It's 'Sunk' With Extra Ballast"
A brand-new 65-foot, 200-passenger excursion boat was too tall to fit under Nine-Mile Bridge. Rather than remove a span of the bridge, the owners sank the boat with heavy ballast until it dropped a foot, then slid it underneath.


========================================
VI. THE SAGA OF THE MARY WORTH (1915)
========================================

21. [1915-07-10 through 07-17] The Mary Worth Debacle
M.M. Egan built a 65-foot pleasure boat and tried to launch it at Lake Worth. On the maiden launch, the stern hit the water too hard, stuck in the mud, and the boat spent two nights underwater. Egan insisted "it was not Mary's fault." After being raised and dried out, the Mary Worth finally sailed -- to great fanfare.
  Quote: "Mary Worth, the bride of Lake Worth and the pride of Fort Worth, is drifting with the gale sailing on the placid waters of the Trinity, having triumphantly passed through the vicissitudes and conquered the hardships of wind and weather on both land and sea."


========================================
VII. FLOODS & THE DAM
========================================

22. [1914-08-16-17] "LAKE WORTH DAM STANDS HARD TEST" / "Blanke Outrides Paul Revere to Reach Dam"
When rumors swept Fort Worth that the dam had broken, Water Commissioner Blanke couldn't get a car through the flooded roads, so he hired a horse and galloped to the dam. He arrived soaked, his clothes ruined, but found the dam holding firm.
  Quote: "Up Main street he galloped, over the Paddock viaduct and out beyond... The dignified official hoisted his legs... as the animal plunged into a creek near... But he got across and on he galloped."


========================================
VIII. RECREATION & SOCIAL LIFE
========================================

23. [1914-06-19] "'Let's Go Fishin' -- Lure for Many Citizens of Ft. Worth"
A wonderful feature story about how 1,200 Fort Worth residents go fishing every weekend and almost none catch anything. Rich and poor sit together in the sweltering sun, sunburned and happy.
  Quote: "Not one in 100 will catch more than a wiggling minnow or two, but they will come home sunburned, limb-weary and there won't be enough fish among the whole bunch to bring 10 cents at the highest market price and they'll raise their hands and swear they won't do it again -- but they'll all be in line by the next Saturday night."

24. [1915-05-16] "Growth of Lake Worth's 'Navy' Proves It Is Fast Becoming City's Most Popular Pleasure Resort"
A census of the lake's fleet: twenty power boats, a hundred canoes and rowboats, one lone sailboat. New clubs forming daily. The article reveals the lake's staggering scale -- practically the same water capacity as a Rhode Island reservoir that cost $9-11 million to build.

25. [1915-07-18] "Mariner Surprised at Lake's Youthful Pilots of Experience"
Two boys, Evanda Haltom and Forrest Gorman, built Lake Worth's only sailboat in a garage in Mistletoe Heights. A visiting marine reporter was astonished at their seamanship.
  Quote: "'All hands fore, now,' shouted the old salt. 'Shift that picture box abaft the hatch. Your Uncle Jack's going to show you lubbers a little close-hauled job.'" The boom lurched, the boat shipped a sea, and the marine reporter gladly surrendered the wheel.

26. [1918-01-06] "Fiddlers Fiddle, Callers Call, Dancers Dance, at Mr. Turner's Lake Ball"
An old-fashioned square dance at the Eagle Club on Lake Worth. Three fiddlers, fifteen couples, and the dance master calling: "Swing your partner! Bow politely!"

27. [1917-07-22] "FIRST LAKE WORTH 'MASHING' CASE IS TRIED; MAN FINED $20"
Will Lloyd, 33, was arrested for addressing unknown girls at the beach with lines like "Come take a trip across the lake with me" and "Cheer up, little girl. Things ain't so bad." He was fined $20. He testified "he meant no harm."


========================================
IX. SCANDALS, LAWSUITS & MONEY
========================================

28. [1914-11-20] "$1,000,000 Claims Filed Against City During Year"
The dam's construction flooded surrounding farmland, ruining crops and washing away cordwood. Farmers demanded nearly a million dollars in damages. The city attorney called their property valuations "ridiculous."

29. [1915-05-29] "CITY WATERWORKS MAKES $37.98 IN THREE MONTHS"
The entire municipal water system turned a quarterly profit of exactly $37.98, on revenues of $62,438.94. The North Side department actually lost $2,610.70.

30. [1917-08-18] "100,000 POUNDS OF FISH FROM LAKE SOLD EACH MONTH"
Despite a city ordinance banning the sale of fish caught in Lake Worth, fishermen were circumventing it by selling their catches just beyond the lake boundary on county land. That is an enormous volume of fish -- 50 tons per month.


========================================
X. WARTIME & THE LAKE
========================================

31. [1918-05-31] "TRADE GERMANY WILL FOR LAKE WORTH SWIM"
A soldier writing from France sent this message home: "I will soon be in a position to exchange the whole German empire for Lake Worth."

32. [1918-03-04 / 04-02] "MAY BAR GERMANS FROM LAKE WORTH" / "ALIEN ENEMIES CAN'T CAMP AT LAKE WORTH"
The city barred "alien enemies" (German nationals) from camping at Lake Worth during WWI, citing security concerns near the water supply.


========================================
XI. MISCELLANEOUS GEMS
========================================

33. [1914-02-16] "VACCINATION OR NO JOB IS ORDER AT LAKE WORTH"
When smallpox broke out among dam construction workers living in tents, the city ordered mass vaccinations.
  Quote: "When a man is sick, the thing to do is to take care of him, not to say it's up to someone else to do so."

34. [1918-01-20] "COYOTE'S HOWL TOLD THIS MAN COLD WAVE DUE"
Road manager Ed Finn heard coyotes singing near the lake early one morning and knew a cold wave was coming before the weather bureau did.
  Quote: "Surest sign in the world. Heard it all my life."
